Another Perspective

I was curious—what hidden connections might exist across 2,000 years of vases? So, I turned to a deep learning model for help. Using clustering, an unsupervised learning technique that doesn’t, the model searched for patterns all on its own. What could it find that I hadn’t noticed?

Clustering is like uncovering secret friendships between vases. It groups them together based on shared traits, but it doesn’t tell you why. Maybe it grouped them by color, shape, or even subtle textures—ultimately, it’s up to us to interpret the results. That’s the beauty (and mystery!) of clustering.

The results were 8 distinct clusters. Scroll down to explore!

Seeing the Data Anew

Cluster 1: Black drinking and serving vessels, mostly Greek, with a few Roman examples of similar rounded forms.

Cluster 2: Focused on decorative motifs, includes full vases and fragments from ancient Greece.

Cluster 3: Earthy-toned vases and figurines, blending Greek and Roman styles from across the Mediterranean.

Cluster 4: Large, utilitarian vessels from Greece, Rome, Britain, and Egypt, with simple, practical designs.

Cluster 5: Stone fragments with functional, undecorated forms.

Cluster 6: Artistic, sculptural vases resembling animals; ceremonial in nature.

Cluster 7: Black vase fragments, likely remnants of cooking or storage vessels.
